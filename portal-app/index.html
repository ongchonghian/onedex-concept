      Concept brief · May 2026
      
    From two systems to one portal
    
      
    Today, operators move between two separate portals — one to set up data-sharing relationships, another to actually exchange data — translating between several different vocabularies along the way. The platform rewrite consolidates this into one portal with one mental model: set up an Agreement with a counterparty once, then exchange Messages under it. The expected outcomes: about 40% fewer clicks on common operator tasks, a single training curriculum instead of two, and a new safety affordance for legally-significant documents that the current system lacks.

    01 Upstream of compose
    
      
    Two ways to set up consent
    
      
    Before any Message can flow, an Agreement has to exist between two parties. The legacy world had 3–4 consent representations to choose between; the new portal has one record with two intents.
    

      
    
        
    
          
    
            Today
            
    Pick the right kind of contract
    
          
    
          
    "I want to set up data sharing with Maersk. Is this a Subscription? A Data Exchange Relationship? A Service Provider Relationship? Let me check the docs first..."

      2.

      5.

      6. 
          
    
          
    The operator has to know the legal taxonomy upfront and pick the right variant. Each has its own behaviour. Picking the wrong one means revoking and starting over.
    
        
    

        
    
          
    
            New portal
            
    Describe the intent
    
          
    
          
    "I want to share Cargo manifests with Maersk."
    
          
      
            
    1. 1Click + New Agreement → pick an intent in plain English: Share data with a counterparty or Appoint a service provider on my behalf
      2. 
            
    2.

After an operator creates 3 similar Agreements, the portal offers them a one-click template for the next one.
    
        
    
      
    

      
    
        
        
    
          
    Several legacy variants → one Agreement
    
          
    Today's system uses several different consent record types to model the same business question — "who can send what data to whom, possibly via a third party?" The new model collapses them all into one Agreement with an internal variant flag that the operator never sees.
    
          
    That is why every downstream activity — composing a Message, finding it in the inbox, auditing it, reconciling with the counterparty — uses the same vocabulary. There is no longer a branching "is this a DER or an SPR?" question at any step. The operator sees Agreement everywhere; the system handles the legal structure.
    
        
    
      
    

      
      
    
        
      
    
    
    

    
    
    
      
    02 The shift, downstream
    
      
    Two ways to think about composing a Message
    
      
    Once Agreements are set up, composing a Message under one is the dominant operator task. Step reduction is the symptom; this is the cause.
    

      
    
        
    
          
    
            Today
            
    Assemble the parts
    
          
    
          
    "I'm allowed to send data on this network. Let me pick who I'm sending to, then pick what I'm sending, then go to the right form for that data type."
    
          
      
            
    1. 1Check that the operator's organisation is permitted to send data on this network
      2. 
            
    2. 2Pick a counterparty from the list of organisations this operator can act for
      3. 
            
    3. 3Pick a data element (the type of record being sent)
      4. 
            
    4. 4The operator has to know which form page to use for that data element — routine documents and legally-transferable records sit on different pages
      5. 
            
    5. 5Fill the form on the right page · submit
      6. 
          
    
          
    Every send is an ad-hoc combination. The operator chooses every dimension each time, including which module to use. Nothing connects this send to a prior consent decision except a global permission.
    
        
    

        
    
          
    
            New portal
            
    Act under a contract
    
          
    
          
    "This Agreement says I send Cargo manifests to Maersk. What's the next Message under it?"
    
          
      
            
    1. 1Open an Agreement — the contract that's already in place
      2. 
            
    2. 
              
      The Agreement carries every dimensionThe counterparty, the data element and its agreed schema, the direction (sending · receiving · staging), the safety level (routine vs legally-significant), and who's authorised to compose (the owner or a service provider acting on their behalf).
      
            
      3. 
            
    3. 2Click + Send Message on the Agreement
      4. 
            
    4. 3Fill the data fields · submit
      5. 
          
    
          
    Composing a Message is acting under a contract that already decided every dimension — the operator just fills the data. The same composer handles routine and legally-significant documents alike, adding a review step automatically when an element is high-stakes.
    
        
    
      
    

      
    
        
        
    
          
    Where did the "pick" steps go?
    
          
    The three "pick" steps from the legacy model — pick counterparty, pick data element, pick form route — are not deleted. They are moved upstream into Agreement creation, where they belong as deliberate consent decisions made once, with the counterparty's explicit acceptance.
    
          
    That is why the same operator action ("send a Cargo manifest to Maersk") takes 5 steps instead of 9 — the dimensions are decided once at Agreement creation and reused for every Message sent under it. Consent and action are decoupled.

    06 Nine decisions that shape the experience
    
      
    Why operators (and admins) will find this simpler
    
      
    The decisions below were made deliberately during design. Each has a clear business consequence for operators and the organisations using the network.
    

      
    
        
    
          
    01
    
          
    Inbox is the home page
    
          
    Operators land on a queue of items needing action — not a generic dashboard. Fewer "where is X?" moments; fewer support tickets about navigation.
    
          
    Inboxes are team-shared, not personal: every operator sees the team's pending work, anyone can claim an item in one click, and a 5-minute completed by [name] echo lingers in colleagues' inboxes so work no longer silently vanishes when someone acts.
    
          
    Customer outcome — faster time to first task; no duplicated effort across the team
    
        
    
        
    
          
    02
    
          
    One page for sent and received data
    
          
    Today's split between "shared data" and "received data" forces operators to know which page their record lives on. The new portal shows everything on one page with filters. The underlying transport details stay invisible.
    
          
    Customer outcome — less training, fewer mistakes
    
        
    
        
    
          
    03
    
          
    One status vocabulary
    
          
    Every Message uses the same four labels — In flight, Delivered, Acknowledged, Failed — regardless of how the data is being exchanged. Compliance reports become straightforward; status discussions stop needing translation.
    
          
    Failed Messages also carry an owner badge — your action, their action, or expired — so the inbox automatically routes only the failures an operator can actually fix to them, and the twice-daily digest never asks anyone to chase a counterparty's problem.
    
          
    Customer outcome — clearer audit trails; failures land with the right person
    
        
    
        
    
          
    04
    
          
    Composing happens under an Agreement
    
          
    Sending data starts on the contract that authorises it. The system pre-applies the counterparty, the data type, and the safety level — operators just fill the data. The safety gradient (routine vs legally-significant) is owned by DEX admins on the data-element catalogue, not toggled by the operator — so individuals can't accidentally downgrade a high-stakes document.
    
          
    Service providers acting on a data owner's behalf get an explicit Acting-as workflow — a chip on the composer names the owner they're composing for, and the audit trail records both the operator and the acting-as org. The compliance question this raises is the first ask below.
    
          
    Customer outcome — fewer clicks, cleaner audit, reduced compliance risk
    
        
    
        
    
          
    05
    
          
    Agreement terms are frozen at acceptance
    
          
    What a counterparty agreed to at acceptance does not change underneath them. If the underlying schema evolves, the Agreement is revoked-and-recreated with the new terms — never silently updated. When an Agreement does end, the timeline records not just that it ended but how — revoked, expired, withdrawn, or auto-terminated — so compliance reviewers get the audit story without separate documentation.
    
          
    Customer outcome — legal certainty; no silent term changes
    
        
    
        
    
          
    06
    
          
    Notifications match the stakes
    
          
    Operators mark time-sensitive Agreements as Watched; they get immediate notifications on completion or failure. Routine failures collect into a twice-daily digest. No notification fatigue; no missed escalations.
    
          
    Customer outcome — operators trust their inbox
    
        
    
        
    
          
    07
    
          
    Multi-counterparty distributions are one gesture
    
          
    When the same data needs to go to several counterparties — a Vessel arrival pack to four logistics partners, say — operators set them up as an Agreement pack in one wizard pass, see them grouped on the Agreements list, and dispatch to all members with one Send pack action. Each pack member stays a fully independent Agreement with its own counterparty, so the audit trail is still per-counterparty and revoking one counterparty doesn't affect the others.
    
          
    Customer outcome — large multi-counterparty distributions take minutes instead of an afternoon
    
        
    
        
    
          
    08
    
          
    Operators describe their own side; the other side stays opaque
    
          
    Larger organisations run multiple Pitstops — operational endpoints that can be sliced by division, branch, geography, or team however the organisation prefers. Today this is a coordination headache: operators on one side have to know how the counterparty has set up their Pitstops to make routing decisions. The new portal turns this asymmetric — each side describes only its own Pitstop topology, and the platform stitches the routing at message-time.
    
          
    For single-Pitstop organisations (the majority) nothing changes — they never see the choice. For multi-Pitstop ones, the operator picks which of their Pitstops dispatches each element exactly once at Agreement creation; from then on it's silent. The counterparty's Pitstop topology never appears in the wizard, the Agreement detail, or the "View as counterparty" panel — only post-facto, as forensic detail on a Message's delivery trace if an operator goes looking. Per-Message forensic detail surfaces when needed; routine work stays uncluttered.
    
          
    Customer outcome — multi-Pitstop orgs gain explicit per-element routing without forcing the rest of the network to learn their setup; operators on both sides only describe what they know about their own organisation
    
        
    
        
    
          
    09
    
          
    Admins author elements themselves, no engineering relay
    
          
    Today, adding a new document type — or revising an existing one — requires a customer-success manager to draft an Excel, a developer to translate it into code, tech leads to manually copy schemas across environments, and the operations team to restart infrastructure. Four actor types coordinate; days pass; engineering is the bottleneck on what is fundamentally a content decision.
    
          
    In the new portal, a DEX admin opens the Data elements catalogue and authors the new document directly. She picks how to start — describe in plain English, drop a sample CSV, upload the legacy PDF or Word form, or fork an existing element — refines the auto-drafted schema with the partner-facing form preview visible beside her, marks the document routine or legally significant, optionally adds cross-field business rules, reviews everything in one place, and publishes. Single screen; single admin; minutes. Snapshot semantics protect every existing agreement automatically: bumping a version creates a new record rather than rewriting the old one, so partners already in flight aren't disturbed.
    
          
    Customer outcome — new document types live the same day they're decided; engineering teams come off the catalogue relay; partners on existing agreements are never disturbed by version bumps

    Three calls before the build begins
    
      
    
        
    
          A
          
    Confirm who can send data on each Agreement
    
          
    The new portal only lets the data owner (or a service provider explicitly acting on the owner's behalf) compose Messages. Today's system is more permissive.     5–6 operators from current customer organisations, walked through 3 sample tasks each. The test validates the two biggest design hypotheses (the consolidated Messages page and the new Composer). Budget: about a week of design time plus an afternoon of operator sessions. The findings could shift design before the larger build investment begins.

        • The Agreement appears in the Ended filter on the list page but the detail page remains readable indefinitely.
